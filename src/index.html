The Asian Elephant

Introduction Physical Characteristics Habitat Cognition Social Behaviour Threats Cultural Significance

The Asian elephant (Elephas maximus), also known as the Asiatic elephant is one of only three surviving species of Elephants, the others being the African forest elephant and the African bush elephant. This majestic animal is found across the Indian subcontinent and south-east Asia, in the following countries – Bangladesh, Bhutan, Cambodia, China, India, Indonesia, Laos, Malaysia, Myanmar, Nepal, Sri Lanka, Thailand and Vietnam. It is the largest living animal in Asia. There are currently three subspecies of the Asian elephant –

  1. Indian (Elephus maximus indica) - This subspecies has the widest range (found over mainland Asia) and accounts for a majority of the remaining Asian elephants.
  2. Sri Lankan (Elephus maximus maximus) - Found in Sri Lanka, this subspecies is the largest in size and the darkest of the three.
  3. Sumatran (Elephus maximus sumatranus) - Found on the Indonesian island of Sumatra, this is the smallest of the Asian elephants

Physical Characteristics

Skin

Asian elephants have grey skin, although there may be some depigmentation, mainly around the ears, trunk and forehead, which generally develops with age. The skin is sensitive as it contains many nerve centers. Elephants protect their skin from heat and even insects by covering themselves with mud and sand, a behavior known as wallowing.

Ears

Asian elephants have large ears which they primarily use to cool themselves in hot climates. The ears consist of a large number of blood vessels. Elephants flap their ears or stand against the wind with their ears spread out to allow the wind to cool their blood which then circulates through the rest of their body, reducing the overall body temperature. Since Asian elephants live in climates that are not as hot as those African elephants live in, their ears are therefore smaller in comparison.

Trunk

One of the most prominent features of an elephant is its trunk, which is an elongation of the nose and upper lip combined, with the nostrils at its tip. It has a small projection at the tip, called a finger, which aids in precision. The trunk does not contain any bones and is entirely made up of muscular and membranous tissue. Elephants use their trunks for a number of activities, like breathing, watering, feeding, touching, dusting, washing, pinching, grasping, defense and offence. The trunk can hold about 5-10 litres of water. For communication, elephants create sounds usually through the larynx, and may use their trunks to modify them. A well known sound of an elephant, the trumpet, is made by blowing through the trunk. Trumpeting is made during excitement, distress or aggression.

Tusks

Another distinct feature of elephants are their tusks, which are modified upper incisors, that grow throughout an elephant’s life. They are composed of ivory, a material similar to bone. Elephants use tusks to dig for water, salt, and rocks, to debark and uproot trees, as levers for maneuvering fallen trees and branches, for work, for display, for marking trees, as a weapon for offence and defense. Asian elephants have much smaller tusks than African elephants, which are called tushes. Many Asian male elephants ay be tuskless.

Habitat

Asian elephants are considered forest animals but also inhabit areas that have intermittent open grasslands as well. They tend to live close to water bodies as they need to drink atleast one in a day. They are highly adaptable and are known to migrate in search of areas with sufficient vegetation and water.

Cognition

Elephants are considered to be one of the most intelligent animals in the world. Their brains are similar to that of humans and other mammals with regard to connectivity and functional areas, although the structure is different. Elephants exhibit a wide range of behaviours like learning, cooperation, altruism, self-awareness, memory as well as emotions such as grief and compassion.

Social Behaviour

Elephants are highly social beings, and maintain tight relationships with their herds. They are matriarchal, with herds mainly consisting of females and calves, while males tend to leave their groups once they reach adolescence and go on to remain solitary or join temporary groups with other male elephants.

"There is no creature among all the Beasts of the world which hath so great and ample demonstration of the power and wisdom of almighty God as the elephant." - Edward Topsell

Threat

Asian elephants are listed as Endangered since 1986 on the IUCN’s Red List. Their numbers around the world are declining, with their population down by about 50 percent over the last 7 decades. This decline in population is mainly due to the following factors –

  • Habitat Loss

    Asia, being the most populous continent, has seen a lot of economic and development growth over the past few decades. To accomodate this, large areas of land that were habitats of Asian elephants have been encroached upon by humans for their settlements, industries, agriculture etc, leading these animals into smaller and smaller forest land.

  • Human-Elephant Conflict

    With the human encroachment of the elephants' natural elephants, many are forced outside of protected areas and into or in close proximity to these developed areas. These can sometimes lead to damage or loss to property and agricultural crops, injuries or death, which in turn leads humans to retailiate back resulting in violent clashes with elephants that can end up in loss of elephant lives.

  • Poaching

    Another major threat to elephant populations, elephants are poached in many areas for ivory along with elephant skin, hair, tail and meat.

    In 1989, the Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora (CITES), a global agreement among governments to regulate or ban international trade in species under threat, banned the international trade in elephant ivory. However, there are still places where the trade is thriving, and unregulated domestic ivory markets in a number of countries fuel the illegal international trade. - WWF

Cultural Significance

The earliest indications of captive use of Asian elephants, dating back to roughly 3000 BC, are engravings on seals of the Indus Valley civilisation. Throughout history, across the Asian subcontinent, elephants have been tamed and used by humans in war as mounts or siege engines, for hunting, as beasts of burden or even help with physical labour to carry objects. Throughout Asia, humans have had a strong association with Asian elephants, so much so that in cultures such as Hinduism, elephants are regarded as a religious and cultural icon. Asian elephants make appearances in the Panchatantra fables and the Buddhist Jataka tales. In hinduism, Lord Ganesha is a reverred diety who has a head of an elephant and a body of a man. During certain festivals, elephants are made to walk in processions adorned with festive outfits.
